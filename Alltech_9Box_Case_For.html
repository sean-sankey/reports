The Case for the 9-Box:
Why It Can Still Work

Companion research briefing for the Alltech workshop, 17 April 2026. A balanced look at the evidence for structured talent review, with case studies of organisations that made the 9-box deliver real results.

Prepared by: Agent Team For: Sean Sankey / Alltech Workshop Date: 14 April 2026 Classification: Internal Prep Companion to: 9-Box Rollout Traps Brief

The companion brief to this document lays out the traps. The evidence is real: Microsoft abandoned stack ranking, GE repudiated the vitality curve, and Deloitte published a landmark Harvard Business Review paper explaining why they walked away from ratings entirely. These are serious organisations making serious arguments.

But here is the thing those stories have in common: they all describe what happens when the tool is misused. When it becomes a labelling exercise, a political process, or a substitute for development. The question for Orla and Robbie is not whether the 9-box can fail. It obviously can. The question is whether it can succeed, and what the conditions for that look like.

The answer, from the research and from practice, is yes. With clear eyes and the right design choices.

The Honest Picture

Before we make the case for, here is the tension laid out cleanly. Both sides have evidence. Presenting this openly builds credibility in the room.

The Case Against

The most cited failures share a common thread: the 9-box was treated as a verdict, not a conversation. Microsoft's stack ranking turned collaboration into competition. GE's vitality curve destroyed psychological safety over a decade. Deloitte found their rating systems measured rater bias more reliably than employee performance.

CEB/Gartner research showed 72% of employees stay in the same box for two or more years, regardless of actual performance change. The grid calcifies.

Sources: Vanity Fair (Microsoft), HR Magazine (GE), Harvard Business Review (Deloitte, 2015), CEB/Gartner

The Case For

The organisations that succeeded with the 9-box treated it as infrastructure for better conversations, not as a ranking tool. They defined potential specifically, linked placements to concrete development actions, and reviewed the grid dynamically rather than annually. The tool gave leadership teams a shared language for talent that replaced gut-feel horse-trading with structured assessment.

McKinsey research found organisations using structured talent review frameworks reported a 20% increase in employee engagement and measurably stronger retention.

Sources: McKinsey, Talent Strategy Group, AIHR, Quantum Workplace

Case Studies: Where It Worked

These are not organisations that got lucky. They are organisations that made deliberate design choices about how to deploy the 9-box, and those choices drove measurable results.

Unilever
FMCG / Global
Success

Unilever adopted the 9-box framework in the early 2000s as part of a broader commitment to building leadership from within. Rather than using it as an annual labelling exercise, they embedded the grid into a continuous talent development cycle. High-potential employees identified through the review received tailored development programmes, stretch assignments, and structured mentoring.

The critical design choice was linking placement to action. Every person identified in the top-right quadrant had a named development plan with timescales and accountability. Every person in the core performer space had visibility of what "next" looked like, not just where they sat today.

Outcome: Unilever consistently fills 80%+ of senior leadership roles from internal talent, one of the highest rates in the FMCG sector. The 9-box is cited internally as a foundational piece of that pipeline architecture.
PepsiCo
FMCG / Global
Evolved

PepsiCo has used some form of the 9-box for over four decades. What distinguishes their approach is that they never stopped iterating on it. HR executives with deep tenure at PepsiCo describe an approach that has been continuously refined to challenge the inherent biases in how leaders think about their people, particularly around what "potential" means in different contexts.

Their most significant evolution was moving from a once-a-year review to a quarterly rhythm, and from a single manager assessment to a multi-source input model. This directly addressed the two biggest failure modes: the frozen label and the potential black box. By reviewing more frequently and from more angles, the grid stayed dynamic.

Outcome: PepsiCo's leadership pipeline is widely benchmarked across the sector. Multiple CEOs of other Fortune 500 companies, including Indra Nooyi's successors and alumni now leading other organisations, were developed through this system.
Siemens
Technology / Industrial
Success

Siemens uses the 9-box as part of its global leadership development strategy across a workforce of over 300,000. Their approach focuses on using the grid to assess capabilities and prioritise development initiatives at scale. In a complex, matrixed organisation, the 9-box provided something no other tool could: a common visual language that worked across geographies, divisions, and cultures.

What made it stick was discipline around the "so what." Every talent review cycle produces named actions, not just named placements. Siemens leadership reviews are structured to spend more time on the development plan than on the assessment itself. The grid is the input. The plan is the output.

Outcome: Siemens reports strong internal succession rates at senior leadership levels and uses the 9-box framework as a core component of their strategic workforce planning process.
McKinsey Client
(Multinational)
Cross-sector
Success

A McKinsey case study documents a multinational company that implemented a structured 9-box system with one critical difference from the norm: they built an active succession planning culture around it rather than treating it as a periodic assessment event. The grid was embedded into regular leadership team conversations, not siloed in HR.

Leadership visibility of the grid meant that development commitments had accountability at the top. When a high-potential was identified, the CEO-level team knew about it and expected to see progress. When a core performer was flagged as a retention risk, the response was structural, not anecdotal.

Outcome: 25% reduction in workforce turnover within the first cycle. McKinsey's broader research found companies using structured talent review frameworks saw a 20% increase in employee engagement.
Why the 9-Box Works When Done Right

The research points to five distinct advantages that structured talent review provides, advantages that alternative approaches (no framework, competency-only models, or purely data-driven systems) struggle to replicate.

Argument 01

It Creates a Shared Language

Before the 9-box, most leadership teams have no common vocabulary for talent. Conversations happen in corridors, based on impressions. The grid forces a structured discussion using shared definitions. "High performance" and "high potential" mean the same thing to everyone in the room, or the process of calibrating them reveals that they don't, which is equally valuable.

The Talent Strategy Group: "It's not about the boxes. It's about the quality of conversation the boxes force you to have."
Argument 02

It Makes the Invisible Visible

In most organisations, talent decisions happen informally and unevenly. The people who get developed are the people who are visible to the right leaders. The 9-box surfaces the entire population systematically. It forces leaders to account for everyone, not just the people who self-promote or who they happen to know well.

AIHR research: the 9-box had a positive influence on employee performance, and this effect was significantly stronger when directly linked to career development activities.
Argument 03

It Connects Assessment to Action

The most powerful feature of the 9-box is not the diagnosis. It is that each box implies a different set of interventions. High performance, high potential: stretch and accelerate. High performance, moderate potential: retain and deepen. Low performance, high potential: investigate and support. Without a framework, development is generic. With one, it can be precise.

Research consistently shows that linking talent assessment directly to differentiated development actions is the single strongest predictor of positive outcomes.
Argument 04

It Scales Across the Organisation

For organisations of any complexity, the 9-box provides something no purely narrative approach can: a common framework that works across teams, functions, and geographies. It allows a global leadership team to discuss talent in Alltech UK and Alltech US and Alltech Asia using the same structure, with the same definitions, and to compare and move talent across boundaries.

Siemens and Unilever both cite the 9-box's scalability as a primary reason for adoption across complex global structures.
Argument 05

It Builds Succession Muscle

Succession planning without structured assessment is guesswork. The 9-box, when reviewed regularly, builds an organisational habit of thinking about talent pipelines. Over time, the conversation shifts from "who can do this job?" to "what do our people need so they are ready when the moment comes?" That shift, from reactive to proactive, is where the real value sits.

McKinsey: companies with structured, regular talent review processes are 2.4x more likely to have "ready now" successors for critical roles than those without.

The Conditions That Make It Work

The research and the case studies converge on a clear set of design principles. Organisations that follow these do not just avoid the traps. They generate measurable improvements in retention, engagement, and succession readiness.

Define potential precisely

Not "could go far" but "able to lead through ambiguity at two levels above current role within a defined timeframe." Specificity kills the bias that vague definitions invite.

Review quarterly, not annually

The frozen label trap disappears when the grid is a living document. PepsiCo's quarterly rhythm keeps assessments dynamic and prevents calcification.

Multi-source input

Never rely on a single manager's assessment. Bring in peer feedback, skip-level perspectives, and objective performance data to triangulate the picture.

Action plan over assessment

Spend 20% of the talent review on placement and 80% on what happens next. If there is no named development action for every box, the exercise was theatre.

Invest in the middle

The core performers are the operational backbone. The organisations that succeed give the middle of the grid structured development, not just the top right corner.

Train the managers first

The grid is only as good as the people using it. Assessment capability, feedback skills, and calibration discipline need to be built before the tool is deployed.

Slide Language Options: "Why This Can Work for Alltech"

Counterpart to the "Traps" slide options. These are designed to land the message that the 9-box is worth doing if done with discipline. Pick the ones that feel right for the room.

Option A

The organisations that walked away were using it wrong. Microsoft ranked people against each other. GE fired the bottom 10%. Those aren't talent development tools. The 9-box works when it drives conversations and actions, not labels and sentences.

Option B

The grid is not the output. The development plan is the output. The organisations that get value from this spend 20% of their time on placement and 80% on what they do about it. The ones that fail do it the other way round.

Option C

Unilever fills 80% of its senior roles from within. They have used the 9-box for over two decades. The difference is that every placement has a named action behind it. They treat it as infrastructure for development, not a classification system.

Option D

The alternative is not "no bias." The alternative is invisible bias. Without a structured framework, talent decisions happen in corridors based on who is visible, who self-promotes, and who the senior team happens to know. The 9-box surfaces everyone. That is its real value.

Option E

The question is not whether the 9-box can fail. It can. The question is what the conditions for success look like. And those conditions are well understood: define potential precisely, review regularly, link every placement to a named action, and invest in manager capability before you deploy the tool.
