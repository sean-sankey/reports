Sources: The 9-Box Rollout Traps

Research cited in the companion brief covering what typically goes wrong with 9-box implementations.

  • Deloitte: "Reinventing Performance Management" (HBR, April 2015)
    Marcus Buckingham & Ashley Goodall. Landmark paper disclosing Deloitte abandoned ratings after finding systems measured rater bias more reliably than employee performance. Replaced annual reviews with quarterly "performance snapshots."
    Full article freely accessible on Harvard Business Review.
  • DeNisi & Murphy: "Performance Appraisal and Performance Management: 100 Years of Progress?"
    Journal of Applied Psychology, 102(3), 421-433, 2017. 30-year review of performance rating research. Concludes calibration processes can amplify rather than neutralise rater biases.
    Abstract on PubMed. Full paper available via institutional access or APA PsycNET.
  • Gallup: "Managers Account for 70% of Variance in Employee Engagement"
    From the "State of the American Manager" report. Based on data from 27 million employees and 2.5 million work units. Establishes that manager quality is the primary driver of engagement variation.
    Full article freely accessible on Gallup.
  • McKinsey & LeanIn.Org: Women in the Workplace 2022
    Study of 333 companies and 40,000+ employees. Documents gender bias in how potential and performance are rated, with particular impact on women of colour's advancement.
    Full report freely accessible. Also available as PDF from McKinsey.
  • Vanity Fair: "Microsoft's Lost Decade" (August 2012)
    Kurt Eichenwald's investigation into Microsoft's stack ranking system, describing it as "the most destructive process in all of Microsoft." Documented how engineers refused to collaborate with talented colleagues for fear of relative ranking.
    Full article PDF hosted at California State University. Original also on Vanity Fair (may require subscription).
  • Deloitte: Global Human Capital Trends (2019)
    Cited for findings on disproportionate development spend on high-potentials versus core performers. Survey of nearly 10,000 respondents across 119 countries.
    Report overview freely accessible. Specific talent investment data referenced from the full downloadable report.
  • CEB/Gartner: Talent Management Research
    Cited for findings that employees tend to remain in the same 9-box position across review cycles, and that tiered talent systems can reduce discretionary effort in lower-rated populations. These findings are from Gartner's proprietary HR research.
    Press release accessible. Underlying data requires Gartner subscription. Widely cited in secondary practitioner literature (AIHR, Quantum Workplace, Culture Amp).
  • RHR International: Succession Readiness Research
    Cited for longitudinal research on the gap between "ready now" succession designations and actual performance when promoted to senior roles.
    Research overview page accessible. Specific study data from RHR's proprietary leadership assessment research.
  • Tomas Chamorro-Premuzic: "The Talent Delusion" (2017)
    Professor of Business Psychology, UCL & Columbia. Research on the weak correlation between managers' subjective potential assessments and actual career outcomes. Argues organisations confuse confidence with competence in talent identification.
    Published book. Available on Amazon. Key arguments also accessible via his HBR articles and talks.
  • Marcus Buckingham & Ashley Goodall: "Nine Lies About Work" (2019)
    ADP Research Institute. Argues talent review systems surface the best-networked and most visible people, not necessarily the most talented. Critiques the assumption that feedback is uniformly valuable.
    Published book. Available on Amazon.
  • Peter Cappelli: Faculty profile, Wharton School
    George W. Taylor Professor of Management & Director of Wharton's Center for Human Resources. Research on high-potential identification failures. His work with Keller (2014) argues there is little reason to believe subjective potential assessments produce valid information.
    Faculty page with links to published research. Key papers available via ResearchGate and Wharton publications.

Sources: The Case For the 9-Box

Research cited in the companion brief covering why the 9-box works when implemented with discipline.

  • Marc Effron / Talent Strategy Group: "It's Not About the Boxes"
    Former VP Talent Management at Avon. Comprehensive argument that the grid is a forcing function for quality talent conversations, not a classification tool. Addresses common objections and makes the case for structured review.
    Full article freely accessible. Also available as downloadable PDF.
  • AIHR: "9-Box Grid: A Practitioner's Guide"
    Academy to Innovate HR. Research finding the 9-box had a positive influence on employee performance, with the effect growing significantly stronger when directly linked to career development activities.
    Full article freely accessible.
  • Quantum Workplace: "A Smarter Approach to Evaluating Performance & Potential"
    Evidence for the 9-box as a tool to move from gut-instinct talent decisions to structured, consistent, data-informed assessment.
    Full article freely accessible.
  • Impact Group HR: "McKinsey 9-Box Talent Matrix: Pros and Cons"
    Secondary source citing a McKinsey case study of a multinational company that reduced workforce turnover by 25% through structured talent review. Also cites McKinsey data on 20% engagement improvement.
    Secondary practitioner source. Cites McKinsey research; original McKinsey report not freely available.
  • myHRfuture: "How Unilever Has Created a Culture of Internal Talent Mobility"
    Podcast and article on Unilever's Flex Experiences platform (65,000 global employees), promote-from-within culture, and pandemic workforce redeployment of 3,000+ people.
    Full content freely accessible.
  • Fuel50: "Internal Mobility: What Is It And Why Organizations Need It?"
    Cited for the P&G statistic: 80% of executive roles filled through internal promotions. Also covers Unilever's internal mobility infrastructure.
    Full article freely accessible.
  • Josh Bersin: 9-Box Grid Analysis
    HR industry analyst. Argues that companies which abandoned the 9-box failed because they used it to judge people instead of develop them. Critiques the assumption that "potential" means "ability to be promoted."
    Blog articles freely accessible. Bersin's deeper research requires Josh Bersin Academy membership.
  • Culture Amp: "Pros and Cons of Using a 9-Box Grid for Succession Planning"
    Balanced practitioner analysis of when the 9-box adds value for succession planning and when alternative approaches may serve better.
    Full article freely accessible.
  • HR Curator: "Humpty Dumpty and the 9-Box"
    Five evidence-based steps to rebuilding the 9-box using the science of leadership potential. Practical implementation guidance for organisations wanting to modernise rather than abandon the tool.
    Full article freely accessible.
  • Gallup: "Employees Want a Lot More From Their Managers"
    Research on continuous feedback and engagement. Companies engaging in continuous feedback have 14.9% lower turnover rates than those relying on periodic reviews.
    Full article freely accessible on Gallup.
  • McKinsey: "The War for Talent" Research
    Foundational McKinsey research on structured talent review. Broader findings on the link between talent management discipline and organisational performance.
    Overview page accessible. Detailed findings from proprietary McKinsey research and consulting engagements.
  • SIGMA Assessment Systems: Internal Promotions at P&G and Novo Nordisk
    Analysis of how structured internal promotion practices at P&G signal organisational stability and strong succession pipeline health.
    Full article freely accessible.
