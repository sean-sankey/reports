How to play it

You have more material than you can use. The mistake to avoid is trying to land all four topics evenly. Better to give Andy three clean "soundbite moments" he can edit toward, and let one topic land deeply rather than four superficially.

Lean into

Topic 1 (Difficult Conversations). Strongest reel territory, and the angle most under-served in agri sector content. It's also the natural way for Andy to draw you out personally, not just as a vendor.

Topic 2 (Compelling Communication). You have the freshest evidence: two deliveries just signed with Alltech. Naming the work is fine. Lean on the framework, not the deal.

Hold back

Don't pitch MorePeople buyers in language they hear from every vendor. No "transformational." No "world-class." No "unlock value." Andy's audience can hear those at any other supplier dinner.

Don't try to be funny if it doesn't arrive naturally. Calm, weighty, specific. The yellow hoodie guy energy. Not the consultant trying to win the room.

The four topics

Each with three Andy questions and three reel-ready one-liners (~30 seconds each).
Topic 01

Why difficult conversations are the most underrated leadership skill in the food and fresh produce world

Pillar A · Difficult Conversations
Frame to set up early
Set this up in your first answer The relationships in this industry outlast the deals. Family businesses, long supplier histories, generational handovers. People manage discomfort by avoiding it. That avoidance is the work. Not because anyone is weak. Because the cost of getting it wrong feels too high.
Questions Andy could ask
  • Sean, you do a lot of work on difficult conversations. Why is this such a thing for our world right now?
  • What's the moment that made you realise this was the work?
  • What do leaders most often get wrong when a difficult conversation lands in front of them?
  • If a listener wanted to start practising this on Monday morning, where do they start?
Reel-ready one-liners
"In this industry, the relationships outlast the deals. That's what makes difficult conversations harder, not easier."
Why it worksNames the agri specificity in 16 words. Leads with a truth Andy's audience already feels but hasn't had named for them. Strong opener line.
"Most leaders confuse being polite with being kind. Polite is what stops the conversation. Kind is what moves it."
Why it worksOne of your sharpest lines. Reframe earned in 19 words. Reels that flip a familiar word land. Pair with a beat of silence.
"The conversation you're avoiding, your team is watching most closely. They've already drawn conclusions about why you haven't had it."
Why it worksSpecific, slightly uncomfortable, lands on the listener personally. The kind of line a senior leader screenshots.
Topic 02

The communication skill that agri leaders pitch with every day but have never been trained for

Pillar D · Compelling Communication
Frame to set up early
Set this up in your first answer Alltech just commissioned two pieces of work on this in the last few weeks. Senior people inside a global business who pitch to retailers, to boards, to internal sponsors all day long, and were never trained for it. They were trained for technical excellence and have been asked, quietly, to perform commercial excellence too.
Questions Andy could ask
  • You've just signed two pieces of work with Alltech on what you call Compelling Communication. What's that actually about?
  • Why is this such a gap for senior leaders in our sector specifically?
  • You've got a framework. Substance, Story, Style. Walk us through it.
  • What changes for a leader when they actually get this right?
Reel-ready one-liners
"Senior agri leaders pitch every day. Most have never been trained for it. They were trained for technical excellence and asked to perform commercial excellence too."
Why it worksNames the audience's lived reality. The pivot from "trained for X" to "asked to perform Y too" is the moment the listener recognises themselves.
"Compelling communication isn't a performance. It's being the most regulated person in the room."
Why it worksDefines the term against what the audience expects (performance, charisma). "Regulated" is the unexpected word that earns the line.
"Substance is what you actually have to say. Story is how you frame it for the people in the room. Style is how you carry it. Most leaders are good at one. You need all three to land."
Why it worksGives the framework cleanly in 35 seconds. Reusable as a standalone explainer reel. This is the IP. Name it in this exact shape, the rhythm matters.
Topic 03

The hardest shift in agri leadership right now. Operator to executive.

Pillar B · Operator to Executive
Frame to set up early
Set this up in your first answer A lot of senior agri leaders came up doing the work. Building the business. Running the operation. Then they get promoted into a seat that asks for something completely different. Most of them keep doing what worked before, and that is exactly the bit that fails them.
Questions Andy could ask
  • You spend a lot of time with senior people moving from operator to executive. What's the hardest part?
  • What's a sign that someone hasn't quite made the transition yet?
  • What gets in the way?
  • Where does this show up in our sector specifically?
Reel-ready one-liners
"The skills that got you promoted aren't the skills that make you good at the new job. Almost everyone tries to keep doing what worked before. That's the bit that fails them."
Why it worksThree short sentences. The third one lands on the listener. Reusable as a 30-second standalone reel.
"An operator solves the problem. An executive makes sure the right people are solving the right problem. The shift looks small. It changes everything."
Why it worksCrisp distinction in 21 words. The "looks small, changes everything" close is reel rhythm.
"Most senior leaders who can't switch off on holiday aren't dedicated. They're afraid the work is too fragile to survive their absence. That fear is the diagnostic."
Why it worksReframe of "loyalty / dedication" as fear. Slightly provocative, lands hard on the listener who recognises themselves. Strong closing line for the topic.
Topic 04

What's actually changing in agri and food leadership right now

Pillar C · Landscape and Pressure
Frame to set up early
Set this up in your first answer A through-line is showing up across the businesses I'm working with this year. Agri leaders are now facing what tech leaders faced ten years ago. More stakeholders. Less time. Fewer places to hide. The job has changed under their feet, and most of them are using leadership instincts from a slower, more predictable era.
Questions Andy could ask
  • When you look across your client base in our world, what's the through-line you're seeing?
  • What's the conversation that keeps coming up?
  • If you could give one piece of advice to a senior leader in our sector today, what would it be?
  • What gives you optimism?
Reel-ready one-liners
"Agri leaders are now facing what tech leaders faced ten years ago. More stakeholders. Less time. Fewer places to hide."
Why it worksDrops a benchmark from outside the sector. Lists the pressures in three beats. Listener self-locates inside it immediately.
"The leaders who'll do well in the next five years aren't the ones who know the most. They're the ones who can have the cleanest conversations under the most pressure."
Why it worksForward-looking line. Connects all four topics back to one principle. This is your closing reel candidate. The line a listener saves.
"If you can name the uncomfortable thing in the room early and well, you're already ahead of most leaders in your industry."
Why it worksPractical close. Names a single skill, makes it competitive, sends the listener off with something to try.
Last things to remember

The four moves that make the edit work for you.

You can't control which 15 minutes Andy picks. You can control how many obvious soundbite moments you give him to pick from, and how often you anchor in a specific scene.

The single line you most want to land

If only one quote makes the final cut, make it: "Most leaders confuse being polite with being kind. Polite is what stops the conversation. Kind is what moves it." Repeat the structure once in a different topic if it doesn't land first time.

Use one concrete scene per topic

A specific room, a specific moment, anonymised. Andy will edit toward the bits with imagery. "A CEO opens a tool, expects confirmation, gets a pattern they didn't know was there." That sort of beat. Vivid beats abstract every time.

Name Alltech once, lightly

Mention the just-signed work on Compelling Communication as evidence, not as a brag. "Alltech just commissioned two pieces of this work." That's enough. Then move to the framework. The work is the proof, not the headline.

Close on the future-looking line

Andy will probably ask a "what's next" or "advice" question near the end. Have the closing reel candidate ready: "The leaders who'll do well in the next five years aren't the ones who know the most. They're the ones who can have the cleanest conversations under the most pressure."
