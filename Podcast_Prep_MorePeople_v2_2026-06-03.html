How to play it

You have more material than you can use. The mistake to avoid is trying to land all four topics evenly. Better to give Andy three clean soundbite moments he can edit toward, and let one topic land deeply.

Lean into

Topic 1 (Difficult Conversations). Strongest reel territory. The "too safe or too dangerous" frame is from your own page copy. The six barriers framework is your actual teaching IP. Use both.

Topic 2 (Compelling Communication, internal teams). Lead with the reframe: most people assume this is pitching for business. It isn't. The lived examples are Chanel internal innovation and Alltech conference speakers. Land "do less, better" as the spine.

Hold back

Don't pitch this audience in vendor language. No "transformational." No "world-class." No "unlock value." Calm, weighty, specific.

Don't reach for aphorisms that don't sound like you. If a line in this doc reads as catchier than your normal voice, soften it on the day. The audience trusts the practitioner, not the pitch.

Topic 01

Why difficult conversations are the most underrated leadership skill in our world

Pillar A · Difficult Conversations
Frame to set up early (anchor in your own page language)
Set this up in your first answer Most leadership teams have conversations that are either too safe or too dangerous, and it's undermining progress on important issues. The work is showing leaders how to name issues with care, hold boundaries early, build trust sincerely, and deal with tension cleanly. So problems get resolved and the work moves forward with momentum.
Questions Andy could ask
  • Sean, you do a lot of work on difficult conversations. Why is this such a thing for our world right now?
  • What's the moment that made you realise this was the work?
  • What do leaders most often get wrong when a difficult conversation lands in front of them?
  • If a listener wanted to start practising this on Monday morning, where do they start?
Reel-ready one-liners
"Most leadership teams have conversations that are either too safe or too dangerous. Both stop the work. Important things don't get said, or the room shuts down. Either way, progress stalls."
Verbatim spineThe "too safe or too dangerous" phrasing is from your own Difficult Conversations page. Naming the binary is the cleanest entry.
"The work is showing leaders how to name issues with care, hold boundaries early, and deal with tension cleanly. The point isn't conflict resolution as a topic. It's that work stops moving until the conversation gets had."
Verbatim spine"Name issues with care, hold boundaries early, deal with tension cleanly" is your own training description. Lifted directly, then closed with the practical why.
"There are six barriers we teach. Most leaders recognise them the moment they hear them. The work isn't learning what they are. It's catching yourself when you're in one."
Why it worksNames your actual IP (six barriers) without listing them on a podcast. Earns curiosity, points the listener toward the training without pitching it.
Topic 02

Compelling Communication is mostly internal. It's calm, concise precision. Not crowding the message with noise.

Pillar D · Compelling Communication · Internal teams
Frame to set up early (this is the reframe most people need)
Set this up in your first answer People assume this work is pitching for new business. It isn't, mostly. The training we just sold into Alltech is for colleagues persuading colleagues, and for senior people speaking at company conferences. The Chanel programme is for internal innovation. The BIG moments we work on are mostly inside the building: presenting to leadership, conference stages, internal show-and-tells, the difficult update no one wants to deliver. The promise is calm, concise precision. Not crowding the message with noise.
Questions Andy could ask
  • You've just signed two pieces of work with Alltech on what you call Compelling Communication. What's that actually about?
  • I think a lot of people assume this kind of training is about pitching to win business. Is that what it is?
  • You've got a framework. Substance, Story, Style. Walk us through it.
  • What changes for a senior leader when they actually get this right?
Reel-ready one-liners
"People assume this work is about pitching for new business. It isn't, mostly. Most of our work is internal. People persuading colleagues. Speaking at the company conference. Presenting to their own leadership team. Pitching an idea inside an innovation programme. The use cases are inside the building, not outside it."
Why it worksThe reframe in one beat. Names the actual use cases (Alltech, Chanel) without selling. Reusable as a standalone reel.
"Communication is what happens in someone else's head. Not what you say. What lands. Most people in a room have never been taught the difference."
Verbatim signature lineYour own line, used across years of training decks. The strongest single sentence in your IP. Use it.
"The discipline I teach more than any other is: do less, better. Most internal communicators crowd the message with noise. The work is cutting it back to what actually has to be there."
Verbatim signature phrase"Do less... better" is on your IP reference as a signature line. Paired with the "crowding the message with noise" framing you just gave me. Lands the promise of the training cleanly.
Topic 03

The hardest shift in agri leadership right now. Operator to executive.

Pillar B · Operator to Executive
Frame to set up early
Set this up in your first answer A lot of senior leaders in this industry came up doing the work. Building the operations, running the business. Then they get promoted into a job that asks for something completely different. Most people keep doing what worked before, and that's the bit that doesn't transfer.
Questions Andy could ask
  • You spend a lot of time with senior people moving from operator to executive. What's the hardest part?
  • What's a sign that someone hasn't quite made the transition yet?
  • What gets in the way?
  • Where does this show up in our sector specifically?
Reel-ready one-liners
"A lot of senior leaders in our sector came up doing the work. They built the operations, ran the business. Then they get promoted into a job that asks for something completely different. Most people keep doing what worked before. That's the bit that doesn't transfer."
Why it worksMeasured, declarative, no aphorism. Sounds like you describing a pattern you've watched, not pitching a frame. Lands on the listener.
"An operator solves the problem. An executive makes sure the right people are solving the right problem. The shift sounds small. It changes the whole job."
Why it worksClean distinction without the consultant-speak. The "sounds small, changes everything" close is your rhythm.
"Most senior leaders who can't switch off on holiday aren't dedicated. They're afraid the work is too fragile to survive without them. That fear is the actual thing to work on."
Why it worksReframes a familiar pattern as something workable. Lands on the listener personally. If you want to soften it on the day, replace "afraid" with "worried." Same meaning, less aphoristic.
Topic 04

What's actually changing in agri and food leadership right now

Pillar C · Landscape and Pressure
Frame to set up early
Set this up in your first answer Across the businesses I'm working with this year, the through-line is pressure. More stakeholders to manage. Less time to think. Fewer places to hide. Agri leaders are now facing what tech leaders faced ten years ago, and most of them are using leadership instincts from a slower, more predictable era.
Questions Andy could ask
  • When you look across your client base in our world, what's the through-line you're seeing?
  • What's the conversation that keeps coming up?
  • If you could give one piece of advice to a senior leader in our sector today, what would it be?
  • What gives you optimism?
Reel-ready one-liners
"Agri leaders are now facing what tech leaders faced ten years ago. More stakeholders. Less time. Fewer places to hide. The job has changed under their feet."
Why it worksDrops a benchmark from outside the sector. Lists the pressures in three beats. The listener self-locates inside it.
"The leaders who'll do well in the next five years aren't the ones who know the most. They're the ones who can have the cleanest conversations under the most pressure."
Why it worksForward-looking line. Connects all four topics back to one principle. This is your closing reel candidate. The line a listener saves.
"If you can name the uncomfortable thing in the room early and well, you're already ahead of most leaders in this industry."
Why it worksPractical close. Names a single skill, makes it competitive, sends the listener off with something to try.
Last things to remember

The four moves that make the edit work for you.

You can't control which 15 minutes Andy picks. You can control how many obvious soundbite moments you give him, and how often you anchor in a specific scene.

The single line you most want to land

"Communication is what happens in someone else's head. Not what you say. What lands." If only one quote makes the final cut, make it this one. It's already your line, it earns its place, and it carries the spine of every training you sell.

Use one concrete scene per topic

Specific room, specific moment, anonymised. Andy will edit toward the bits with imagery. "A senior colleague is preparing to speak at a global company conference. They've got forty slides. We get them to seven." Vivid beats abstract every time.

Name Alltech and Chanel once, lightly

The point of naming them is to show the work is real and internal-facing, not external pitching. "Alltech just commissioned two pieces of this. Their use case is colleagues persuading colleagues, and people speaking at the company conference." Then move to the framework. Evidence, not headline.

Close on the future-looking line

Andy will probably ask a "what's next" or "advice" question near the end. Have the closing reel candidate ready: "The leaders who'll do well in the next five years aren't the ones who know the most. They're the ones who can have the cleanest conversations under the most pressure."
