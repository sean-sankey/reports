2 The Frame

Hybrid: discovery-shaped, prepared to price if asked.

Every question does two jobs. Scope the population AND surface the story. Do not choose between them.

3 The Read

A Tesco long-term partnership is a commercial award. In return, Tesco expects open-book costing, continuous improvement, Scope 3 sustainability, and a governance story that stands up to their own board.

When Tesco says "too many old people, where's the future coming from," they are naming a supplier resilience risk. A category-critical grower whose top layer sits in a small window of five to ten years is a fragile link. It becomes an ESG story their investors read.

If unanswered: volume rebalances quietly toward a co-supplier over 18-36 months, partnership terms tighten, category pricing power softens.

This lives at the ownership and executive table. Not HR. Not L&D. The intervention needs credibility with a retailer, not just an HR outcome.

5 Hooks Bank
Green: Opening Statements Amber: Provocative Questions Blue: Pattern Recognition
Opening Statements (signal understanding early)
01 "Tesco don't put you into a long-term partnership because your carrots are cheapest. They do it because they need you to still be there in ten years, and both sides of that deal have to believe it."
02 "The businesses I work with in this shape usually have three or four people who effectively are the business, and they are all inside the same fifteen-year window. That is not a leadership problem, that is a continuity problem."
03 "The conversations we tend to have start in HR and end at the ownership table, because that is where the answer actually lives."
04 "A retailer partnership like the one you have is worth serious money, and it comes with obligations that get inspected. Supplier resilience is one of those obligations now, and it wasn't five years ago."
05 "Most fresh produce businesses I know are one or two people leaving in the same year away from a serious problem. What helps is naming those people and getting on the front foot before the market notices."
Provocative Questions (urgency without accusation)
01 "If Tesco asked you tomorrow to walk them through your generational plan, what would you actually show them?"
02 "Who in this business, if they left in the next 18 months, would give you a real problem? How many of those names are there?"
03 "When you and John talk about the next five years, are you talking about the same business? Or do you have slightly different pictures in your heads about who owns it, who runs it, and where the family sits?"
04 "How much of what makes TH Clements work sits in people's heads rather than in a system, a document, or a second person who could do it?"
05 "If we designed the next 12 months well, what would your NAM at Tesco see that they don't see today?"
Pattern Recognition (the "he gets us" moments)
01 "The pattern I see in businesses like this is that ownership and executive control blur across the family, and no one has drawn the line explicitly. Not because it is hidden, but because there has never been a reason to draw it. The retailer pressure is usually the reason."
02 "In every family agri-business I've worked with, the founder generation carries eight to twelve years of institutional knowledge that has never been written down. When they step back, that knowledge is what moves the value of the business up or down, and it is what determines whether a Tesco partnership survives the handover."
03 "The businesses that navigate this well do three things: they name successors early, they let them do real work in front of the customer inside 18 months, and they build a governance structure that separates ownership decisions from operating decisions. The ones that don't are sold, absorbed, or restructured on someone else's terms."
04 "There is almost always a middle layer, five to eight people, who could carry more and aren't being asked to. Retailers see that gap before the business sees it, because retailers are talking to the same three people every quarter."
05 "The commercial director sitting in a conversation like this is usually the tell. It means the business has already decided this isn't an HR project. They just haven't quite said it out loud yet."

10 Watch-Fors
Kayle lesson: don't reach for frameworks

Convert "leadership development" into two or three recent moments where better leadership would have changed the outcome, BEFORE offering any MC tool. Teach less, leave with more to sell.

Do NOT name Tesco unless they name Tesco first

Andrew told Sean in confidence. If Sean names them first, Andrew's position in the room collapses. Wait for them to open the door, then walk through.

Don't turn this into a leadership training pitch

The temptation is to translate "generational renewal" into "we run a great leadership programme." The moment Sean does that, he loses the room.
